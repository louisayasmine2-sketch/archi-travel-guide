# Best Siena Hotels with Parking for Drivers and Tuscany Day Trips

> **How we made this list:** No hotel paid to be included, and nothing here is sponsored. Parking costs and conditions were checked against hotel and official sources in July 2026, and the caveats are the ones we would want to read before booking.

If you are arriving in Siena with a rental car and plan to use the city as a launchpad for day trips into Chianti, the Val d'Orcia or San Gimignano, parking is not a minor detail. It is usually the single biggest practical headache of the trip.

Siena's historic centre sits inside a strictly enforced ZTL (*zona a traffico limitato*). Street parking is scarce, the cameras are unforgiving, and plenty of highly rated central hotels have no parking of their own at all. Choose the wrong base and the car costs you time, money and patience before you ever reach Piazza del Campo.

This guide covers only hotels that solve the car problem. Every property below has private parking that is either free, reliably bookable, or valet-handled — and we have listed what each one actually costs. **Parking rates and conditions were checked against hotel and operator sources in July 2026.** Rates change; confirm at booking.

For neighbourhood character and pure pedestrian recommendations, see [LINK: Where to Stay in Siena]. For wider planning, use the [LINK: Siena Travel Guide] and [LINK: Tuscany Travel Guide].

## First, understand the ZTL — it decides everything

Siena's ZTL is not a suggestion. Cameras read your plate at each gate, and because you are driving a rental, a fine typically reaches you months later via the rental company, which adds its own administration fee on top.

Here is what actually matters for a driver with a hotel booking:

**Hotels can register your plate.** Every property in this guide is used to doing it. You give them your registration and arrival date, they log it with the local authority, and the camera pass is authorised for your entry and exit. This is routine — but it is not automatic. Send your plate number ahead of arrival rather than assuming reception will sort it at check-in.

**Use the hotel's own route, not your satnav's.** This is the part travellers get wrong. Your navigation app will happily route you through a gate your permission does not cover. Every hotel below publishes arrival directions naming a specific city gate; follow those instead. Hotel Athena, for example, directs guests in via Porta San Marco and along the city wall to its lots.

**A permit covers you, it does not make the streets wider.** The historic centre is medieval. Lanes are narrow, turns are tight, and hotel car parks are frequently retro-fitted courtyards. A compact car is genuinely the better choice here — Athena says as much to its own guests, and notes that vans and motorhomes may be charged extra or turned away.

**Your parking window is tied to check-in and check-out.** Several hotels can only guarantee a space between check-in and check-out time. Arriving at 10am with a 2pm check-in, or wanting to leave the car until an evening train, may mean paying separately or parking elsewhere. Ask when you book.

## Hotel Athena — best overall balance of free parking and walkability

Hotel Athena sits inside the historic walls near Porta San Marco. It offers **free parking to all guests across two private outdoor lots and one covered garage** — the most substantial free parking of any hotel this close to the centre, and the reason guests recommend it so consistently.

The walk to Piazza del Campo takes roughly 8–12 minutes, with an uphill stretch on the way back. Rooms are comfortable and well kept rather than luxurious. Breakfast is solid, and the panoramic terrace earns its reputation for an evening drink over the valley.

Useful detail most guides miss: **the parking includes EV charging** — a charging station at the private lot plus two more columns in the garage — but you must flag it when booking.

**Pros:** Free parking that genuinely works, short walk to the main sights, helpful staff, strong value for the location, EV charging on site.

**Caveats:** Spaces are private and subject to availability, and availability is tied to check-in and check-out times — parking before check-in or after check-out is chargeable and not guaranteed. Manoeuvring room is limited; larger vehicles may incur a surcharge. Some rooms feel dated next to newer boutique properties.

**Parking:** Free for guests. **Price band:** typically €150–260 per night in summer (higher around the Palio).

**Practical tip:** Send your plate and arrival time ahead, request EV charging if you need it, and if you are arriving before 2pm ask reception where to leave the car in the meantime.

## Palazzo Ravizza — central garden hotel with private parking

Palazzo Ravizza is one of very few properties combining a central address with on-site parking and a garden terrace looking straight out over the countryside. Family-run since 1922, it sits about 700 metres from Piazza del Campo — call it 8–12 minutes on foot, with the usual Sienese incline on the return.

**Parking costs €10 per day and cannot be reserved in advance.** That matters more than the price. Spaces are allocated first-come, and while the hotel reports the lot rarely fills, you cannot lock one in. Plan an earlier arrival rather than a late one.

**Pros:** Rare combination of centre, parking and genuine garden views; strong period character; excellent for couples who still want a car.

**Caveats:** Parking is paid, limited and unbookable. Access requires following the hotel's ZTL instructions precisely. Rooms vary considerably in size and style — a frescoed ceiling in one, a plain box in another.

**Parking:** €10 per day, no reservation possible. **Price band:** €160–280 in high season.

**Practical tip:** Ask the hotel to email its arrival map and plate-registration form when you book, and aim to arrive early in the day for the best shot at a space.

## Hotel Santa Caterina — garden views just outside Porta Romana

An 18th-century villa a few steps outside Porta Romana, Hotel Santa Caterina offers **private on-site parking at €15 per day, and here reservation is required** — so book the space when you book the room. Breakfast is served on a terrace with a long view across the Tuscan hills, which is most of the appeal.

The walk into the centre runs 10–15 minutes. Being just outside the gate means you avoid the tightest streets entirely while still strolling in.

**Pros:** Attractive garden setting, parking you can actually reserve, well-reviewed breakfast, friendly small-hotel atmosphere.

**Caveats:** Not as central as Athena or Ravizza. The car park is narrow — guests routinely report staff parking the car for them, which is helpful but tells you something about the space. Rooms are comfortable, not luxurious.

**Parking:** €15 per day, reservation required. It is worth asking about the rate if you book direct with the hotel. **Price band:** €130–220.

**Practical tip:** The Via Roma public car park sits close by if the hotel lot is full — useful backup for an early arrival.

## Hotel Italia — practical value with the most flexible fallbacks

Hotel Italia sits in a quiet residential area nearer the train station. Its private car park holds **ten cars at €10 per day, reservation required**, valid from 2pm on arrival day to midday on departure. What makes it genuinely useful for drivers is what happens when that lot is full: there is **free street parking through the surrounding residential streets**, and free parking at its sister property, Hotel Garden, about 600 metres away.

The walk to Piazza del Campo is 15–20 minutes, helped by the escalator link up from the station area.

**Pros:** Three separate parking options at three price points, good breakfast, easy in-and-out for the *superstrada*, sound value.

**Caveats:** Furthest walk of the walkable options. The private lot is small and must be booked. If you use the free street parking, **read the street-cleaning signs carefully** — the hotel itself warns that the police do not go easy on fines. Some rooms are in annexe buildings.

**Parking:** €10 per day private (10 spaces, reserve ahead); free public streets nearby; free at sister Hotel Garden, 600 m. **Price band:** €110–190.

**Practical tip:** Best pick if you want a car park you are not anxious about. Even a full lot leaves you two free alternatives within walking distance.

## Sangallo Park Hotel — best pure road-trip base with a pool

Sangallo Park sits on the Vico Alto hillside, roughly a 10-minute drive from the historic centre, with **free private parking** and an unusually generous amount of it. There is a seasonal outdoor pool with a view over the hills, a garden of olive trees, and quick access to the Siena–Florence *superstrada* — which is the real point if your itinerary is more countryside than city.

Buses and taxis connect to the old town; a taxi runs around €10.

**Pros:** Free, plentiful, stress-free parking. Pool and garden. Excellent highway access for multi-day Tuscany itineraries. Consistently praised staff.

**Caveats:** Not walkable to the sights in any realistic sense — you will drive, bus or taxi every day. **There is little in the way of restaurants nearby and no evening dining at the hotel**, so plan to eat in Siena or drive out. Rooms are modest in size, and some bathrooms have tub-only showers. The pool is seasonal.

**Parking:** Free. **Price band:** €100–180.

**Practical tip:** Ideal if you are spending more days in the Val d'Orcia and Chianti than in Siena itself. Book dinner in town before you head in — you will not want to improvise on the way back.

## Hotel Certosa di Maggiano — luxury monastery base in the countryside

A Carthusian monastery founded in 1314 and converted into a Relais & Châteaux hotel in 1978, Certosa di Maggiano lies about 2 km from Piazza del Campo, with **free private parking**, an outdoor pool, a tennis court, extensive gardens and only fifteen rooms. Interiors were designed by Renzo Mongiardino, and the restaurant is a destination in its own right. It is the most distinctive stay anywhere near Siena.

The centre is a short drive or taxi ride, and guests report a shuttle into town.

**Pros:** Exceptional setting and service, free parking, pool and grounds, genuinely memorable.

**Caveats:** Not walkable. Significantly higher price point. A 14th-century building means limited lift access in places and a more formal atmosphere than some travellers want. Listings differ on whether pool and tennis access is included in every rate — confirm what your booking covers.

**Parking:** Free. **Price band:** €300–550+.

**Practical tip:** Best for travellers who want the countryside atmosphere as much as the city, and are treating the hotel as part of the trip rather than a bed near it.

## Grand Hotel Continental Siena — luxury central option with valet parking

The Grand Hotel Continental (Starhotels Collezione) occupies a 17th-century palazzo between Piazza del Campo and Piazza Salimbeni — the only five-star inside the walls, and a few minutes' walk from everything. **Parking is valet-handled at a nearby facility, published at roughly €55–65 per day, with no reservation needed.** This is the pay-for-maximum-convenience option.

**Pros:** Outstanding central location, high service standards, historic building, perfect once the car is parked and you are walking everywhere.

**Caveats:** Parking is expensive and off-site — and off-site can mean genuinely away from the hotel. Guests have reported collecting the car at a pickup point some distance out, with unclear directions and a long wait. If you have an early departure or a train to catch, build in extra time and get the collection point confirmed in writing at check-in.

**Parking:** Valet, approximately €55–65 per day. **Price band:** €300–500+ in high season.

**Practical tip:** Ask at check-in exactly where and how the car is returned, and how much notice the valet needs. Do not leave it to departure morning.

## If the hotel car park is full: Siena's public parking, decoded

Even with a booked space, you will occasionally need a fallback — an early arrival, a full lot, a Palio week. Siena has over 4,000 spaces in managed facilities, and knowing three things saves real money.

**Escalators do the hill for you.** The San Francesco, Santa Caterina and La Stazione car parks are all fitted with escalators up into the centre. Park at the bottom, ride up. This is the single most useful fact about parking in Siena, and most visitors never learn it.

**Ask your hotel about the discounted hotel rate.** The central facilities (Il Campo, Il Duomo, San Francesco, Santa Caterina) charge around €2 per hour and €35 for a full day at standard rates — but a reduced hotel rate of about €25 for a maximum of one day is published for guests. Your hotel arranges it.

**The cheapest option is not central, and that is fine.** The Eliporto–Policlinico car parks are free for the first hour and around €2.50 for a full day. Stadio–Fortezza runs about €26 a day; La Stazione is roughly €2 beyond the second hour for the whole day, and free on Monday mornings during the weekly market.

For on-street bays outside the ZTL, expect around €1.50 per hour, charged 08:00–20:00 **including weekends and public holidays**, payable at meters or through the Sipark, EasyPark or Dropticket apps. The nearest street car parks to hotels in this guide are Via Roma (near Porta Romana, for Santa Caterina) and Via Bastianini / Via Mascagni (near Porta Laterina, for Athena).

*Rates are those published by Siena's official tourism service and are subject to change — check before you rely on them.*

## Frequently asked questions

### Is it hard to park a car in Siena's historic centre?

Yes. The ZTL is camera-enforced, street parking inside the walls is minimal, and many central hotels have no parking at all. A hotel with its own or partner parking removes nearly all of the stress, because the hotel registers your plate for authorised ZTL access. The remaining job is yours: use the hotel's arrival route rather than your satnav's.

### Which Siena hotels have free parking?

Hotel Athena is the strongest option inside the walls, with two private lots and a covered garage free to all guests. Outside the walls, Sangallo Park Hotel and Certosa di Maggiano both offer free private parking. Hotel Italia gives you a free fallback — public streets nearby and free parking at its sister Hotel Garden, 600 metres away.

### When is the Palio, and how early should I book?

The Palio runs **twice** every summer: the Palio di Provenzano on **2 July** and the Palio dell'Assunta on **16 August**. In 2026 those fall on Thursday 2 July and Sunday 16 August. Both create a sharp spike in demand and rates, and each is preceded by four days of trials and processions, so the pressure covers roughly 29 June–2 July and 13–16 August. Book months ahead; parking-equipped hotels are among the first to sell out.

### Is it better to stay outside the walls if I have a car?

It depends on the split of your trip. Outer properties (Sangallo Park, Certosa di Maggiano, Santa Caterina) make driving and daily departures easier. Walkable hotels with parking (Athena, Palazzo Ravizza) let you park once and leave the car until you need it. If more than half your days are countryside days, stay outside.

### Can I do day trips to Chianti or the Val d'Orcia from these hotels?

Yes — all of them work as bases. Outer hotels reach the *superstrada* faster, but the central ones with parking still allow easy morning departures once you know your exit route. If you have not booked a car yet, [compare rental rates for Tuscany](/go/car-rental) — and pick the smallest vehicle your luggage allows.

### Are the walks from the walkable hotels flat?

No. Siena is built across three hills and the streets are cobbled. Even a 10-minute walk involves uphill stretches and uneven surfaces, and the return leg is almost always the climb. Comfortable shoes are not optional.

### What size car should I rent for Siena?

The smallest that fits your luggage. Hotel car parks in the historic centre are converted courtyards with limited manoeuvring room, and larger vehicles can face surcharges or be refused. A compact car is also far easier on Tuscany's back roads.
